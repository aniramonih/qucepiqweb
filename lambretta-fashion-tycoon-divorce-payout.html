Lambretta Fashion tycoon made to hand over 2.7m to his ex-wife over a decade after they split afte

A TEACHER who built a £30 million fashion empire from his garage must pay his ex-wife £2.7 million - ten years after they divorced.

Glen Briers started up his sporting and streetwear empire Lydenford Ltd in 1998 with just £81 of his own cash.

The 61-year-old split with fellow teacher Nicola Briers in 2002 after 18 years of marriage when the business was turning over £1 million a year.

It has since grown into a huge fashion chain incorporating well-known brands Lambretta and Vision Streetwear and makes a £30 million profit.

When the couple split, Briers gave his wife, 58, £150,000 to pay off the mortgage and let her keep their £700,000 family home.

She also got a £10,000-a-year salary, plus child maintenance for their three children - but he kept the business.

He claims they made a "verbal agreement" at the time, but after her new relationship broke down, the ex-wife went to the divorce courts demanding more.

He was ordered to pay a £2.7 million slice of his £10 million fortune last year made up of a £1.6 million lump sum and a quarter of his pension.

The decision was upheld today at the Court of Appeal after Briers begged judges to overturn his ex-wife's "windfall", claiming it was unfair on him and left her "in a position which is excessively favourable."

Judge Sir Ernest Rider said the businessman was guilty of "deception of the wife and untruthfulness in evidence".

Part of the evidence he gave in 2015 was  "extraordinary, incredible and curious", the Judge added.

Nicola had played a vital role in building up the business during its infancy and was entitled to a stake in its success.

Judge Mark Rogers, who presided over the case two years ago, had branded Briers a "liar" who resorted to "deception" to downplay his wealth.

After their separation, he told his "vulnerable" wife he was moving into a rented home - hiding the fact that he owned it - and also left her feeling "bullied and intimidated" when he failed to fully disclose his assets meaning the financial deal was not worth the paper it was written on.

The agreement had been "driven" by her "dominant" and "psychologically controlling" ex, Judge Rogers explained.

The tycoon had painted his ex-wife's account of negotiations following their separation as "pure fantasy and a cock-and-bull story".

Judge Rogers "decided that the husband was lying" and that the truth was that there had never been any meaningful negotiations at all.

During the latest case, the three senior judges heard that the pair wed in 1984 and have three children who are now grown up.

In 2002 the couple separated and Briers left the family home. They were divorced in 2005.

He says there was an agreement to make a clean break and that divorce judge, Mark Rogers, was wrong to treat the business as "an undivided matrimonial asset."

Jonathan Cohen QC, for Mr Briers, told the Appeal Court it was unfair for him to now have to share his cash over a decade after the split.

He added: "The husband started the business with just £81 of his own money.

"No family money went into the business at all...the wife's involvement in the business was negligible.

"Her explanation as to why she eventually did make a claim, so many years after the event, was that she had heard that the husband may have had another child before his relationship with the wife; that he had a young girlfriend; her own wages as a teacher had been cut back, and she had parted from her boyfriend, with whom she had been in a relationship since the breakdown of the marriage."

But Mr Cohen added: "It is not the husband's role to act as insurer against these events, let alone to do so many years after the separation.

"This was not an undivided asset - the total assets had been divided, with the husband receiving the company and the wife the bulk of the remaining assets.

"The judge has provided the wife with a windfall which has no logical justification.

"It places her in a position which in all the circumstances is excessively favourable."

The court heard that Brier's wife made no contribution to the business, and that he had actually offered her an extra £562,000 to settle her claim.

The husband was a dominant personality who sought to prevail in the relationship psychologically and the wife perceived herself as bullied and intimidated

Justin Warshaw QC, for Nicola Briers

Justin Warshaw QC, for Nicola, insisted the payout should be allowed to stand, and that no legal settlement between the former couple had ever been finalised.

He also claimed that "dominant personality" Briers had "bullied and intimidated" his wife while they were married.

He added that Mrs Briers deserved her payout due to her "huge contribution" in caring for the children while the company grew.

Outside court in November, Briers said: "It's just very disappointing. The business went from a turnover of about £1 million when we split to £30 million.

"I paid for everything. I gave her everything that she wanted and that we agreed on. She got a house valued at £700,000.

"I didn't drag her up to the solicitors to get the agreement signed - we'd been married for years.

"I didn't think she would do anything like this years later."
